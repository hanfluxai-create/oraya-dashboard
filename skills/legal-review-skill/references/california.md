# California / US Jurisdiction Reference
## Legal Framework as of March 2026

---

## GOVERNING LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### Core Contract Law
- **California Civil Code**: Governs contracts in California. Civil Code § 1550 (essential elements of a contract), § 1619-1625 (consideration), § 1636-1656 (interpretation rules), § 1667-1670.5 (unlawful contracts and unconscionability)
- **California Uniform Commercial Code (Cal. UCC)**: Governs sale of goods; Article 2 for goods contracts, Article 9 for secured transactions
- **Restatement (Second) of Contracts**: Widely adopted in California courts for contract interpretation
- **Common law**: California follows common law contract principles supplemented by Civil Code

### Company & Securities Law
- **California Corporations Code**: Governs California corporations. Key provisions: § 25102 (securities exemptions), § 204 (articles of incorporation requirements), § 300+ (board governance), § 1001 (shareholder approval for major transactions)
- **Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL)**: Most startups and large companies incorporate in Delaware for flexibility. Delaware Court of Chancery is the most sophisticated business court in the US.
- **Securities Act of 1933 (federal)**: Governs issuance of securities; Regulation D exemptions (Rule 504, 506(b), 506(c)) for private placements
- **Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (federal)**: Governs trading of securities, disclosure obligations for public companies
- **JOBS Act, 2012**: Regulation Crowdfunding, Regulation A+ for smaller raises

### Employment Law (California is strict)
- **California Labor Code**: Among the most employee-protective in the US. Key: minimum wage, meal/rest breaks, overtime, classification of workers (employee vs. independent contractor)
- **AB 5 (2019)**: Strict test for independent contractor classification (ABC test). Misclassification is extremely expensive — presumption of employment in California.
- **WARN Act (federal + California WARN)**: Advance notice required for mass layoffs
- **FEHA (Fair Employment and Housing Act)**: Anti-discrimination and harassment law; broader than federal Title VII

### Privacy & Data
- **California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA, 2018) + California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA, 2020)**: Comprehensive consumer privacy rights; applies to businesses meeting size thresholds. Rights to know, delete, opt-out of sale of personal information.
- **CalOPPA**: Online privacy policy requirements

### Real Property
- **California Civil Code §§ 1940-1954.06**: Residential tenancy
- **Commercial leases**: Largely governed by contract; California has fewer protections for commercial tenants than residential

### IP
- **Copyright Act (federal, 17 U.S.C.)**: Automatic copyright protection on creation; registration required for lawsuit
- **Lanham Act (federal, 15 U.S.C.)**: Federal trademark law
- **California Uniform Trade Secrets Act**: Trade secret protection; requires reasonable steps to maintain secrecy
- **Patent Act (federal, 35 U.S.C.)**: Patent protection

## NON-COMPETE CLAUSES IN CALIFORNIA

**California Business and Professions Code § 16600**: Non-compete clauses in employment contracts are **void and unenforceable** in California, with very narrow exceptions. California has the strictest anti-non-compete law in the US.

**AB 1076 (2024)**: Employers must notify current and former employees that existing non-compete clauses are void. Violation = unfair competition.

**What IS enforceable in California**:
- Non-solicitation of clients (narrowly, if directly tied to trade secrets)
- Confidentiality and trade secret protection
- Non-compete tied to sale of a business (§ 16601 — buyer can impose non-compete on seller)
- Non-compete in dissolution of partnership

**Practical implication**: If an agreement has a non-compete clause and California law applies, the clause is essentially worthless. Do not rely on it.

## STARTUP & VENTURE INVESTMENT TERMS (California)

### Common Investment Structures
- **SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)**: Y Combinator standard; converts to equity at next priced round; no interest, no maturity date. Review: valuation cap, discount rate, MFN clause, pro-rata rights.
- **Convertible Notes**: Debt instrument converting to equity; has interest rate, maturity date, conversion discount. Review: maturity date (what happens if no qualified financing before maturity?), interest rate, conversion mechanics, most favored nation.
- **Series A/B/C Preferred Stock**: Priced equity rounds with full term sheets. Key terms: pre-money valuation, liquidation preference (1x non-participating is standard; 2x or participating preferred is aggressive), anti-dilution protection, pro-rata rights, information rights, board seats.

### Term Sheet Key Terms
- **Liquidation preference**: How much investors get before common shareholders in a sale or liquidation. 1x non-participating = investor gets back 1x investment OR converts to common; participating preferred = investor gets 1x PLUS participates in remaining proceeds (very investor-friendly; negotiate against)
- **Anti-dilution**: Full ratchet (most aggressive — any down round adjusts all previous investors) vs. weighted average (standard — takes into account size of down round)
- **Drag-along**: Investor right to force founders/common shareholders to approve a sale
- **Pay-to-play**: Existing investors must participate in future rounds to maintain anti-dilution and other protections
- **Information rights**: Monthly/quarterly financials, annual audited accounts, budget, right to inspect books
- **Pro-rata rights**: Right to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership percentage

### NVCA Model Documents
The National Venture Capital Association (NVCA) publishes model term sheets, voting agreements, investor rights agreements, and right of first refusal agreements. These are the industry standard in US venture deals. Always benchmark any proposed terms against NVCA standards.

## CONTRACT INTERPRETATION RULES (California)

California courts interpret contracts using these principles:
1. **Plain meaning rule**: Words given their ordinary meaning
2. **Ejusdem generis**: General words following specific words are limited to the same category as the specific words
3. **Contra proferentem**: Ambiguities construed against the drafter — this is a strong reason to draft clearly
4. **Integration / parol evidence rule**: If contract is fully integrated (entire agreement clause), prior negotiations cannot be used to vary written terms
5. **Good faith and fair dealing**: California implies a covenant of good faith and fair dealing in every contract — this is stronger than Indian law, meaning a party cannot act to frustrate the other's reasonable contractual expectations even if technically permitted by the literal text

## DISPUTE RESOLUTION (US/California)

### Litigation
- **Federal courts**: For federal law claims (securities, patents, federal employment), diversity jurisdiction (parties from different states, amount > $75,000)
- **California state courts**: Superior Court for most commercial disputes; Appellate Court, then California Supreme Court
- **Delaware Court of Chancery**: For Delaware corporations — most sophisticated business court in the US; routinely handles M&A disputes, fiduciary duty claims
- **Timeline**: US litigation 3-7 years to trial; very expensive (discovery costs alone can be millions for complex commercial cases)

### Arbitration (strongly preferred for commercial disputes)
- **AAA (American Arbitration Association)**: Most common; Commercial Rules for business disputes; Consumer Rules for consumer contracts
- **JAMS**: Premium arbitration service; expensive but often faster; common in major commercial deals
- **ICC**: For international deals
- Key provisions: number of arbitrators, seat, governing rules, discovery limitations, confidentiality of proceedings, class action waiver
- **California note**: California courts are hostile to arbitration clauses in consumer contracts that are unconscionable; for B2B this is less of an issue

### Choice of Law
- California courts will enforce choice of law clauses unless the chosen state has no substantial relationship to the parties or the chosen law violates a fundamental California policy
- **Non-compete clauses**: California courts frequently refuse to enforce non-competes even if the contract says another state's law governs, because non-enforceability is a fundamental California public policy

## NDA / CONFIDENTIALITY AGREEMENTS (California)

Standard provisions:
- Definition of confidential information (broad + specific examples)
- Exclusions: already public, independently developed, received from third party without restriction, required to be disclosed by law
- Obligations: use only for permitted purpose, protect with reasonable care (at least same as own confidential info), limited disclosure to need-to-know personnel
- Duration: 2-5 years is standard; some provisions (trade secrets) should survive indefinitely
- Return/destruction of materials on termination
- Remedies: irreparable harm acknowledgment (important for getting injunctions quickly without having to prove damages)
- Governing law and jurisdiction

**One-way vs mutual**: If only one party is sharing information, one-way NDA is fine. If sharing is bilateral, use mutual NDA — a one-way NDA in a bilateral sharing situation leaves one party unprotected.

## COMPLETE CHECKLIST: CALIFORNIA COMMERCIAL AGREEMENT

**Identity & Execution**
- [ ] Legal names of all parties (for corporations: exact corporate name, state of incorporation)
- [ ] Principal place of business addresses
- [ ] Execution date
- [ ] Authorized signatories with corporate authorization reference (board resolution or officer certificate)
- [ ] Consider e-signature (DocuSign etc. — valid under California UETA and federal E-Sign Act)

**Substantive Provisions**
- [ ] Recitals — commercial background
- [ ] Definitions — every capitalized term defined
- [ ] Scope of services/obligations — specific, measurable, avoid vague deliverables
- [ ] Payment: amount, schedule, method, late payment interest (California law allows parties to agree on interest rate; default legal rate is 10% per annum for commercial transactions)
- [ ] Tax: specify whether amounts are exclusive of applicable taxes; who bears sales tax / use tax
- [ ] IP ownership: who owns what is created under the agreement (work-for-hire doctrine in California has nuances — independent contractors in California do NOT automatically transfer IP unless signed written agreement)
- [ ] Representations and warranties
- [ ] Limitation of liability clause (cap on damages): CRITICAL — without this, exposure is unlimited. Standard: cap at total fees paid in prior 12 months; exclude direct damages from cap; exclude IP infringement and confidentiality breaches from cap
- [ ] Exclusion of consequential/indirect damages: "IN NO EVENT SHALL EITHER PARTY BE LIABLE FOR INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, SPECIAL, EXEMPLARY, OR CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES" — must be in caps or bold to be enforceable
- [ ] Indemnification: mutual or one-sided; specify what triggers indemnity and any caps
- [ ] Confidentiality (see NDA section above)
- [ ] Termination rights: for cause (with cure period) and for convenience (30-60 days notice standard)
- [ ] Survival clause: which provisions survive termination (confidentiality, IP, indemnity, dispute resolution)

**Boilerplate**
- [ ] Governing law: "This Agreement shall be governed by the laws of the State of California, without regard to conflict of laws principles" (or Delaware for Delaware corporations)
- [ ] Dispute resolution (arbitration clause if desired — specify AAA or JAMS rules, seat, number of arbitrators)
- [ ] Venue / jurisdiction: specify county in California (San Francisco, Santa Clara, Los Angeles, etc.)
- [ ] Entire agreement / integration clause
- [ ] Amendment: in writing signed by both parties
- [ ] Waiver
- [ ] Severability
- [ ] Counterparts / electronic signatures
- [ ] Notices: email + physical address, deemed receipt timing
- [ ] No agency / no partnership
- [ ] Assignment: can either party assign without consent? Typically "no assignment without prior written consent, not to be unreasonably withheld, except to affiliate or in connection with merger/acquisition"
- [ ] Force majeure (especially post-COVID — specify what qualifies, notice requirements, duration, termination right if FM continues)

## TOP TRAPS SPECIFIC TO CALIFORNIA

**1. Non-compete clauses**: Void under § 16600. Do not include them. If the other party insists, push for trade secret and non-solicitation protection instead — these are enforceable.

**2. Worker classification**: If using independent contractors for California-based work, apply the ABC test (AB 5). Misclassification = back taxes, penalties, benefits liability. Very expensive mistake.

**3. No limitation of liability clause**: Without a cap, damages exposure is theoretically unlimited. Always negotiate a liability cap.

**4. Missing IP assignment**: Work created by independent contractors does not automatically belong to the hiring company in California. Always include explicit IP assignment clause.

**5. Implied covenant of good faith**: California courts will find a breach of the implied covenant even if the express contract terms were technically followed but the conduct was clearly unfair. Draft to avoid situations where this could be argued.

**6. Class action waiver in arbitration**: Generally enforceable in B2B contracts; potentially not enforceable in B2C contracts after California Supreme Court decisions. Specify this carefully.

**7. CCPA/CPRA compliance**: If the agreement involves processing California consumer data, CCPA/CPRA compliance obligations must be addressed in the contract (data processing agreement provisions, consumer rights handling).

**8. Choice of law vs. California public policy**: Even if contract says another state's law governs, California courts will apply California law on non-competes, worker classification, and other fundamental policies.

**9. Attorney's fees clause**: California follows the "American rule" — each party bears own legal fees unless the contract or statute provides otherwise. If you want attorney's fees in case of breach, include a fee-shifting clause. Also note: California Civil Code § 1717 makes attorney's fees clauses mutual even if only drafted in one direction.

**10. Equity compensation in California**: If issuing options or equity to California residents, Blue Sky laws (California securities law, § 25102 exemptions) apply in addition to federal securities law. Always get securities counsel sign-off.
