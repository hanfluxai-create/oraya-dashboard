# India Jurisdiction Reference
## Legal Framework as of March 2026

---

## CORE COMMERCIAL STATUTES

### Contracts & Enforcement
- **Indian Contract Act, 1872**: Foundation of all contract law. Section 10 (valid contract requirements), Section 27 (void non-competes — non-compete clauses are largely unenforceable post-employment in India), Section 29 (uncertain agreements void — "mutually agreed" terms are not enforceable), Section 56 (impossibility of performance — a party cannot be held liable for obligations legally impossible for them to perform), Sections 73-75 (damages — Indian courts have discretion to reduce penalties)
- **Specific Relief Act, 2018 (amended)**: Specific performance (forcing actual performance rather than just paying damages) is now the rule, not exception, for immovable property and certain commercial contracts. Critical for mining rights, land, unique assets.
- **Transfer of Property Act, 1882**: Governs transfers of immovable property, leases, mortgages
- **Registration Act, 1908**: Documents transferring immovable property above INR 100 must be registered. Unregistered = inadmissible as evidence.
- **Stamp Acts (central + state-specific)**: Inadequately stamped documents are inadmissible until penalty stamp duty paid (up to 10x in some states). Always verify state-specific stamp duty requirements.
- **Information Technology Act, 2000 (Section 10A)**: Electronic contracts and digital signatures are valid.

### Company Law
- **Companies Act, 2013**: Section 2 (definitions), Section 56 (share transfer — Form SH-4 required), Section 62 (further issue of shares — right of existing shareholders), Section 186 (inter-corporate loans/investments), Sections 230-240 (mergers and acquisitions). Key: SHA provisions conflicting with Articles of Association (AoA) are unenforceable — SHA must be reflected in or aligned with AoA.
- **Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016**: What happens if investee company becomes insolvent. Investor ranks as financial or operational creditor depending on investment structure.

### Dispute Resolution
- **Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 (amended 2019, 2021)**: Section 7 (arbitration agreement must be in writing), Section 11 (arbitrator appointment), Section 34 (grounds to set aside — limited to procedural grounds, public policy), Section 36 (enforcement). The seat of arbitration determines which High Court supervises — seat is more important than venue.

### Foreign Investment
- **FEMA, 1999**: All foreign investment governed by FEMA. Share issuance to foreign investors below fair value is a FEMA violation. Mandatory RBI reporting.
- **FDI Policy (Consolidated, 2020)**: Coal mining — 100% FDI under automatic route (Press Note 4/2019). Border-sharing countries (China, Pakistan, etc.) require government approval (Press Note 3/2020).

### Sector-Specific: Mining
- **MMDR Act, 1957 (amended 2021)**: Section 11A for coal block allocation via auction
- **Coal Mines (Special Provisions) Act, 2015**: Coal auction framework; constitutes Nominated Authority
- **Coal Blocks Allocation Rules, 2017**: Rule 8(10) — cancellation triggers for Allocation Order
- **Forest Conservation Act, 1980**: Mandatory forest clearance before using any forest land for mining
- **Environment Protection Act, 1986**: Environmental clearance framework via MoEFCC
- **PESA, 1996**: In Scheduled (tribal) Areas — Gram Sabha (village assembly) consent is a legal prerequisite that no government authority can override. Applies to significant parts of Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, MP.
- **LARR Act, 2013**: Land acquisition compensation and Social Impact Assessment requirements

### Tax
- **Income Tax Act, 1961**: TDS obligations, withholding tax on foreign payments, capital gains on share transfers
- **GST Act, 2017**: Services attract GST; always specify whether contract amounts are inclusive or exclusive of GST — missing this creates disputes

## MOU-SPECIFIC RULES IN INDIA

MOUs can be binding or non-binding — language determines which:
- **Binding language**: "shall," "must," "agrees to," specific consequences for breach, consideration already paid
- **Non-binding language**: "intends to," "proposes to," "subject to further agreement"

**Critical**: Once money has been paid under an MOU, Indian courts treat it as part performance creating equitable rights — even a "non-binding" MOU becomes partially binding for amounts paid.

**Confidentiality and exclusivity clauses within an MOU are almost always binding** regardless of whether the MOU itself is non-binding — they are treated as independent enforceable obligations.

**"Mutually agreed" terms are void** under Section 29 — any commercial term left as "to be mutually agreed later" is unenforceable. Always replace with a defined term or a defined mechanism (independent valuer, formula, etc.).

## SHAREHOLDER AGREEMENT ESSENTIALS (India)

Every Indian SHA must cover:
- **Pre-emption / ROFR**: Right of first refusal on any share transfer — in SHA AND reflected in AoA
- **Anti-dilution**: Full ratchet or weighted average — specify clearly which applies
- **Tag-along**: Minority right to exit on same terms as majority
- **Drag-along**: Majority right to force minority to sell — specify threshold % required
- **Reserved matters**: List of decisions requiring investor consent (new funding, asset sale, change in business, related party transactions)
- **Information rights**: Monthly/quarterly financials, audit access, board observer rights
- **Exit mechanism**: IPO timeline, strategic sale right, buyback with defined price formula and timeline
- **Valuation methodology**: How shares are valued for any exit, buyback, or dispute — specify independent valuer + methodology
- **Deadlock resolution**: What happens if board is deadlocked (especially in 50-50 JVs)

## DISPUTE RESOLUTION FRAMEWORK (India)

### Arbitration Best Practices
- Always prefer institutional arbitration over ad hoc: MCIA (Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration), DIAC (Delhi International Arbitration Centre), SIAC (Singapore) for international deals
- Specify: seat (determines supervising court) + venue (physical hearings) — these can be different
- 3 arbitrators for significant commercial disputes (above INR 5 crore)
- Include: language of arbitration, governing rules, timeline for making award
- International deals: Singapore (SIAC) or London (LCIA) as seat is common and enforceable in India under New York Convention

### Jurisdiction for Indian Courts
- Prefer Delhi or Mumbai for commercial disputes — better commercial courts, faster timelines than smaller cities
- Delhi has a dedicated Commercial Court under Commercial Courts Act, 2015 — significantly faster than regular civil courts
- Push back against the other party's home city as exclusive jurisdiction unless commercially justified

### Timeline Reality
- Indian civil courts: 7-15 years to final judgment
- Arbitration (institutional): 18 months to 3 years
- Interim injunction: Days to weeks, but commonly contested

## COMPLETE CHECKLIST: EVERY INDIAN COMMERCIAL AGREEMENT

**Identity & Execution**
- [ ] Full legal names + CIN numbers for all companies
- [ ] Registered addresses
- [ ] Date and city of execution
- [ ] Authorized signatory names, designations, board authorization reference
- [ ] Witness signatures where required by law
- [ ] Appropriate stamp paper for the state of execution (verify state-specific stamp duty)
- [ ] Registration if immovable property rights involved

**Substantive Provisions**
- [ ] Recitals — background and purpose
- [ ] Definitions — every important term defined at first use
- [ ] Clear obligations of each party with specific timelines
- [ ] Payment terms: amount, currency, account details, due date, interest on late payment (specify rate and whether simple or compound)
- [ ] GST treatment explicitly stated (inclusive or exclusive)
- [ ] Representations and warranties from each party
- [ ] Consequences of breach of warranties
- [ ] Indemnity: who indemnifies whom, for what, any caps on indemnity
- [ ] Confidentiality: scope, duration (survive termination?), permitted disclosures, consequences of breach
- [ ] Specific termination triggers for each party (not just mutual consent)
- [ ] Notice period for termination
- [ ] Consequences of termination: refunds, survival of obligations, return of materials
- [ ] Force majeure: definition, notification requirement, duration limit, effect on payment obligations
- [ ] Change of control restrictions if relevant

**Boilerplate (Required)**
- [ ] Governing law: "This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of India"
- [ ] Dispute resolution clause (arbitration preferred — specify seat, venue, rules, number of arbitrators)
- [ ] Jurisdiction clause with specific city
- [ ] Notices: addresses, email, method of delivery, deemed receipt timing
- [ ] Entire agreement (supersedes all prior discussions)
- [ ] Amendment: requires written consent of both parties signed by authorized representatives
- [ ] Severability
- [ ] Waiver clause
- [ ] No agency / no partnership declaration
- [ ] Counterparts (if signing in multiple copies)

## TOP TRAPS SPECIFIC TO INDIA

**1. Inadequate stamp duty**: Document is inadmissible in court. Always check state-specific rates.

**2. Non-compete clauses**: Section 27 Contract Act makes post-employment and many post-contract non-competes void. Limit scope aggressively.

**3. "Mutually agreed" terms**: Unenforceable under Section 29. Replace with formula or independent determination mechanism.

**4. Personal guarantee without cap**: Directors giving unlimited personal guarantees face personal insolvency risk under IBC if company defaults.

**5. SHA vs AoA conflict**: SHA provisions override AoA only between the parties — third parties and courts enforce AoA. Must align both documents.

**6. Penalty clause**: Indian courts can reduce penalties under Section 74 if they find the amount extravagant relative to actual damages. Draft as "genuine pre-estimate of loss" with supporting rationale.

**7. Missing GST clause**: Always state whether prices are GST-inclusive or exclusive; also specify who bears any future tax changes.

**8. Jurisdiction in other party's city**: Practical disadvantage — push for Delhi or Mumbai for major commercial contracts.

**9. FEMA valuation for foreign investment**: Shares cannot be issued to foreign investors below fair value determined by a registered valuer. Document the valuation before issuance.

**10. PESA in tribal areas**: Gram Sabha consent is not just a formality — refusal is legally valid and cannot be overridden. Projects in Scheduled Areas must account for this in timelines and risk assessments.
