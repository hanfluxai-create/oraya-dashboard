# LEGAL REVIEW: MoU for Channel Partnership — Drone Sales
## Vyomik Innovations Pvt Ltd (Party 1) vs. Channel Partner (Party 2)

**Review Date:** March 17, 2026
**Document:** MoU_Patna_CP_v3.docx
**Client:** Party 2 (Channel Partner)
**Jurisdiction:** India — Indian Contract Act 1872, Companies Act 2013, Arbitration & Conciliation Act 1996
**Deal Stage:** Pre-execution (Draft v3)
**Reviewer:** Claude (Legal Analysis Skill — Senior Counsel Framework)

## 1. PLAIN ENGLISH SUMMARY

This MoU sets up a channel partnership where you (Channel Partner / Party 2) will market, promote, and sell Vyomik's agricultural drones across India. You do the fieldwork — find customers, run demos, close deals. Vyomik supplies the drones and provides training. The partnership is non-exclusive (Vyomik can appoint unlimited other partners in your same territory), runs for 5 years, and critically, **all actual commercial terms — your pricing, margins, payment terms — are deferred to future agreements that don't exist yet.** This means you're committing your time, reputation, and operational resources without any guaranteed financial return.

---

## 2. BALANCE ASSESSMENT

**VERDICT: Heavily biased in favor of Vyomik (Party 1).**

This MoU asks you to invest significant resources — staff time, travel, customer relationships, market development — while giving you almost nothing enforceable in return. Vyomik retains full control over pricing, territory, exclusivity, and can terminate at will with 90 days notice after you've built their market. The few protective clauses you do have (product liability in 7.3, travel reimbursement in 11) are good but don't offset the fundamental imbalance: **you build the market, Vyomik owns it.**

---

## 3. PROBLEM ANALYSIS

### PROBLEM 1: No Enforceable Commercial Terms — You Work for Free

**The problem:** Clause 5 defers ALL commercial terms (pricing, margins, payment, credit) to "separate written agreements, purchase orders, invoices, or commercial annexures." None of these exist yet. There is no minimum margin, no pricing formula, no commission structure — nothing.

**Why it matters:** Under Section 29 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, agreements where essential terms are "to be mutually agreed" are void for uncertainty. This means you have zero enforceable right to any specific margin or payment. Vyomik can offer you terms you can't accept, and you have no legal recourse.

**What could go wrong:** You spend 6 months building a pipeline of 50 farmer leads. When it's time to place orders, Vyomik quotes you a price that leaves you a 2% margin — barely enough to cover your costs. You refuse. Vyomik says "take it or leave it." You have no contractual right to any specific margin. Vyomik then appoints another partner (which they can do freely under Clause 3) and hands your leads to them.

**The fix:** Add a Commercial Terms Schedule (Annexure A) to this MoU that specifies: (a) minimum Channel Partner margin as a percentage of MRP (e.g., 15-20%), (b) maximum price at which Vyomik will supply drones to you, (c) payment terms (e.g., 30 days from invoice), and (d) a mechanism for price revisions with 60-day advance notice and your right to reject revised prices without penalty.

---

### PROBLEM 2: Non-Exclusive Territory = Zero Protection for Your Market Investment

**The problem:** Clause 3.1 makes this a "non-exclusive channel partnership." Clause 3.2 explicitly says Vyomik can appoint additional dealers/distributors/partners "anywhere in India or any part thereof." Clause 4 confirms "no exclusive territorial rights."

**Why it matters:** You are being asked to invest time, money, and relationships into developing a market for Vyomik's drones — with no protection against Vyomik flooding that very market with competitors. This is commercially irrational for you.

**What could go wrong:** You spend a year building relationships with FPOs and cooperatives in Bihar. You conduct demos, train farmers, build trust. Vyomik then appoints three more partners in Bihar, each offering the same product. Your customer pipeline is diluted. Worse, a competing partner undercuts your pricing (since there's no minimum margin) and closes deals with your contacts.

**The fix:** Negotiate for either: (a) exclusive territory for at least your primary operating states (e.g., Bihar, Jharkhand) for a defined period (minimum 2 years), with performance milestones that must be met to retain exclusivity; OR (b) at minimum, a "right of first refusal" — Vyomik must offer you the opportunity to service any lead in your operating area before appointing another partner there; AND (c) a "lead protection" clause — any customer you introduce remains your account for 12 months regardless of which partner ultimately closes the sale.

---

### PROBLEM 3: No Minimum Supply Obligation from Vyomik

**The problem:** Vyomik's obligations under Clause 2.1 are soft: "supply drones at mutually agreed commercial terms," "provide product training," "extend standard warranty as per company policy." There is no obligation for Vyomik to actually supply ANY drones to you, nor any minimum quantity commitment.

**Why it matters:** Without a minimum supply commitment, Vyomik can starve you of inventory. You bring customers, but Vyomik has no obligation to fulfill your orders — they could prioritize their direct sales channel or preferred partners.

**What could go wrong:** You close a deal with a major FPO for 100 drones. Vyomik says they have supply constraints and can only give you 10, while fulfilling orders for their direct customers. You lose the FPO relationship and your credibility.

**The fix:** Add: "Vyomik shall fulfill confirmed purchase orders from the Channel Partner within [30] days of order acceptance. Vyomik shall not unreasonably refuse or delay acceptance of purchase orders. In the event of supply constraints, Vyomik shall allocate available inventory to all channel partners proportionately based on confirmed orders."

---

### PROBLEM 4: Termination Without Protection for Your Investment

**The problem:** Clause 9.2 allows either party to terminate with just 90 days' notice. Clause 9.3 says "termination shall not affect obligations accrued prior to termination" — but this only covers past obligations. There is zero protection for your future business, customer relationships, or market investment.

**Why it matters:** After building Vyomik's brand and customer base in your territory, Vyomik can terminate the MoU and immediately appoint a cheaper partner to service the customers YOU developed. All your investment walks out the door.

**What could go wrong:** After 2 years, you've built a customer base of 200 farmers and 15 FPOs. Vyomik terminates the MoU, hires a local agent at half your cost, and hands them your customer list (which Vyomik has access to through your order records). You're left with nothing.

**The fix:** Add post-termination protections: (a) "Upon termination, the Channel Partner shall be entitled to receive commission on all sales to customers introduced by the Channel Partner for a period of 12 months following termination." (b) "Customer lists and relationships developed by the Channel Partner shall remain the proprietary business information of the Channel Partner." (c) "Vyomik shall not directly or through another partner solicit customers introduced by the Channel Partner for 12 months following termination." (d) A termination compensation formula tied to the Channel Partner's trailing 12-month revenue contribution.

---

### PROBLEM 5: One-Sided Compliance Burden (Clause 7.1 & 7.2)

**The problem:** Clause 7.1 makes YOU solely responsible for all regulatory compliance — DGCA regulations, trade laws, state/local requirements. Clause 7.2 makes you solely liable for ALL representations to customers and any unauthorized commitments. Vyomik is completely shielded from liability for your actions.

**Why it matters:** Drone regulation in India is complex and evolving (DGCA drone rules, UAS rules, state-specific permissions). You're being asked to bear ALL compliance risk for selling a product you don't manufacture and whose regulatory certifications are Vyomik's responsibility. If a DGCA certification is wrong or incomplete — that's Vyomik's fault, but under 7.2 you could still be dragged in.

**What could go wrong:** You sell a drone to a farmer relying on Vyomik's product documentation stating it's DGCA-compliant for agricultural spraying. DGCA later finds the certification was incomplete. The farmer gets fined. Under Clause 7.2, you're liable for "representations made to customers" — even though you relied on Vyomik's own documentation.

**The fix:** Clause 7.3 partially addresses this (Vyomik is liable for defective products and incorrect documentation), which is good. But add explicit language: "The Channel Partner's liability under Clause 7.2 shall not extend to representations made in reliance on product documentation, certifications, specifications, or marketing materials provided by Vyomik. The Channel Partner shall be liable only for representations that materially deviate from Vyomik's approved documentation."

---

### PROBLEM 6: Advance Payment Exposure (Clauses 12 & 13) — Dangerous Default

**The problem:** Clause 12 requires 50-50 sharing of all advance payments (security deposits, earnest money, arbitration deposits, etc.) for government tenders. Clause 13 says if fault is "disputed, shared, or indeterminate," loss defaults to 50-50.

**Why it matters:** Government tender deposits in India can be substantial — INR 5 lakh to INR 50 lakh or more. You're being asked to put up 50% of these deposits with no control over the product quality, tender compliance, or delivery that determines whether the deposit is returned.

**What could go wrong:** You jointly bid on a Bihar government tender for 500 agricultural drones. You put up INR 15 lakh as your 50% share of the earnest money deposit (EMD). Vyomik fails to deliver on time due to manufacturing issues. The government forfeits the EMD. Vyomik claims the delay was partly because you didn't provide adequate demand forecasting. Fault is now "disputed" — under Clause 13, you lose your INR 15 lakh.

**The fix:** (a) Add: "The Channel Partner shall not be required to contribute more than INR [X] lakh in aggregate Advance Payments at any point in time without specific written consent." (b) Revise the fault allocation: "Where an Advance Payment is forfeited due to product defect, manufacturing delay, or any failure attributable to Vyomik's supply obligations, Vyomik shall bear 100% of the loss, regardless of dispute." (c) Add: "No Advance Payment shall be made by the Channel Partner without a prior written agreement specifying the transaction, the proportions, the recovery mechanism, and the timeline."

---

### PROBLEM 7: Confidentiality Clause is Incomplete (Clause 8)

**The problem:** The confidentiality clause is bare-bones. No defined term (how long does it last?), no specific remedies for breach, no carve-outs for information that becomes publicly available, and no survival clause post-termination.

**Why it matters:** Under Indian law, confidentiality obligations in an MoU are binding even if the MoU is otherwise non-binding. But an incomplete clause creates ambiguity. Also, it's one-sided in practice — you'll share customer data and market intelligence, while Vyomik shares pricing and product specs. Your data is harder to protect because it's generated from your fieldwork.

**The fix:** Add: (a) Term: "Confidentiality obligations shall survive termination for a period of 3 years." (b) Exclusions: "This clause does not apply to information that is or becomes publicly available through no fault of the receiving party, was already in the receiving party's possession, or is required to be disclosed by law." (c) Remedies: "The disclosing party shall be entitled to seek injunctive relief in addition to damages for any breach." (d) Return of information: "Upon termination, each party shall return or destroy all confidential information of the other party within 30 days."

---

### PROBLEM 8: Arbitration in Ranchi — Questionable Choice

**The problem:** Clause 10.2 specifies Ranchi, Jharkhand as the seat and venue of arbitration. Vyomik is based in Hyderabad. Ad hoc arbitration (not institutional) with a sole arbitrator.

**Why it matters:** Per the India reference framework: (a) Ad hoc arbitration is slower and less predictable than institutional arbitration (MCIA, DIAC). (b) The seat determines which High Court supervises — Jharkhand High Court is not known for commercial dispute efficiency. (c) Sole arbitrator for a 5-year commercial partnership may be insufficient.

**The fix:** (a) Change to institutional arbitration — MCIA (Mumbai) or DIAC (Delhi) for better procedural frameworks. (b) Consider changing seat to your home city or a neutral commercial hub (Delhi or Mumbai). (c) If deal value justifies it, use 3 arbitrators instead of 1.

---

### PROBLEM 9: Structural Defects — Duplicate Numbering

**The problem:** The MoU has TWO Clause 11s (Travel/Accommodation and Amendment) and TWO Clause 12s (Advance Payments and Entire Understanding). This is a drafting error that creates ambiguity in any future dispute when referring to clause numbers.

**The fix:** Renumber. The correct sequence should be: 11 (Travel), 12 (Advance Payments), 13 (Limitation of Liability), 14 (Amendment), 15 (Entire Understanding).

---

### PROBLEM 10: Missing Essential Clauses

The following clauses are standard for Indian commercial agreements and are completely absent:

**Force Majeure:** No provision for what happens if performance is disrupted by events beyond control (natural disasters, government orders, pandemics, supply chain disruption). Without this, you remain liable for obligations even during force majeure events.

**Representations & Warranties:** Vyomik makes no representations about its legal authority, product quality standards, DGCA certifications, financial standing, or absence of conflicting agreements. You're taking them at face value.

**Indemnity:** No indemnification framework. If a third party sues you because of Vyomik's product defect (beyond what 7.3 covers), there's no mechanism for Vyomik to make you whole.

**Severability:** No clause saying that if one provision is found invalid, the rest survives. One bad clause could void the entire MoU.

**Notice Provisions:** No specified method for delivering notices (email? registered post? courier?), no deemed receipt timing. A termination notice could be "lost" with no consequence.

**Waiver Clause:** No provision saying that failure to enforce a right doesn't mean waiving it permanently.

**GST Treatment:** No mention of whether any payments under this MoU are inclusive or exclusive of GST. Per the India reference, this is a top trap that creates disputes.

**Stamp Duty:** No mention of which party bears stamp duty costs. Inadequately stamped documents are inadmissible in Indian courts.

## 4. NON-NEGOTIABLE RED LINES

These must be fixed before signing. Walk away if Vyomik refuses:

**RED LINE 1 — Enforceable Commercial Terms.** A minimum margin or pricing formula MUST be attached as a schedule. Without it, you have a 5-year obligation to sell drones with no guaranteed income. This is a dealbreaker.

**RED LINE 2 — Territory Protection.** At minimum, exclusive rights in your primary operating states with performance milestones, plus lead protection for customers you introduce. Without this, you're building a market for someone else to harvest.

**RED LINE 3 — Post-Termination Compensation.** Commission tail on sales to your customers for 12 months after termination, plus non-solicitation of your customers. Without this, Vyomik can terminate and take your customer base for free.

**RED LINE 4 — Advance Payment Cap and Fault Allocation.** An aggregate cap on your advance payment exposure, and clear allocation that makes Vyomik bear 100% of losses caused by their product or supply failures.

**RED LINE 5 — Compliance Liability Carve-Out.** Your liability for customer representations must be limited to deviations from Vyomik's approved documentation. You cannot be liable for relying on their own materials.

## 6. MISSING CLAUSES (Must Be Added)

**Force Majeure** — Without this, you're liable even when performance is impossible due to government orders, natural disasters, or supply chain collapse. Standard in every Indian commercial agreement.

**Representations & Warranties** — Vyomik should warrant: valid incorporation, authority to enter this MoU, valid DGCA certifications for all products, no conflicting agreements with other partners in your territory, and financial capacity to fulfill supply obligations.

**Indemnity** — Vyomik should indemnify you for: product defects, incorrect certifications, product liability claims from end users, and any IP infringement claims related to the drones.

**Severability** — If any clause is found invalid, the rest of the MoU survives.

**Notice Provisions** — Method of delivery (registered post + email), deemed receipt timing (48 hours for email, 7 days for post), addresses for notices.

**Waiver** — Failure to enforce a right is not a permanent waiver.

**GST** — All prices exclusive of GST; each party responsible for its own GST compliance.

**Stamp Duty** — Each party bears stamp duty on its own copy; Vyomik bears cost of the original.

**Minimum Supply / Order Fulfillment** — Vyomik must fulfill confirmed orders within a defined timeframe.

**Performance Review** — Annual review mechanism with defined KPIs rather than unilateral termination right.

**What drives this score:**

The MoU is not a scam or a trap in the malicious sense — Clauses 7.3 (product liability on Vyomik), 11 (travel reimbursement), and the mutual arbitration framework show good faith. However, the fundamental commercial structure is heavily stacked against you:

- **No enforceable margins** (+15 points of risk) — the single biggest issue. You could work for years with no guaranteed income.
- **No territory protection** (+12 points) — your market investment is unprotected.
- **No post-termination protection** (+10 points) — your customer base can be taken.
- **Advance payment exposure** (+8 points) — open-ended financial commitment with weak fault allocation.
- **Missing standard clauses** (+7 points) — force majeure, indemnity, reps & warranties, severability all absent.
- **One-sided compliance burden** (+5 points) — partially mitigated by 7.3.
- **Structural defects** (+3 points) — duplicate numbering, no definitions section.
- **Arbitration concerns** (+2 points) — ad hoc in Ranchi instead of institutional.

**Positive factors reducing the score:**
- Clause 7.3 (product liability on Vyomik) is strong and well-drafted (-8 points)
- Clause 11 (travel reimbursement) is generous and enforceable (-6 points)
- Mutual termination rights with 90-day notice (-3 points)
- Clear arbitration mechanism exists even if venue is suboptimal (-2 points)

**Bottom line:** This MoU has a solid skeleton and some genuinely good protective clauses for you. But the commercial heart of the deal — how you get paid, whether your territory is protected, and what happens when it ends — is either missing or tilted against you. Fix the five red lines and add the missing clauses, and this becomes a workable partnership agreement.
