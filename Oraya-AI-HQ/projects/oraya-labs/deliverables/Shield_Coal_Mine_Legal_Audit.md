## 3. THE REGULATORY FRAMEWORK — WHAT THE LAW ACTUALLY SAYS

### 3.1 The Lock-in Period (CMDPA Clause 13.1.3 / Tender Document Clause 5.2.1.3)

Cholapathar is a **Partially Explored Mine** (only 20% GR exploration completed, categorised G-2 or G-3 per Indian Standard Procedure for Coal Resource Estimation, 2017). For Partially Explored Mines, the lock-in has **two phases**:

**Phase 1:** From the date of issuance of the Allocation Order until **one year after issuance** (i.e., October 10, 2025 → October 10, 2026), PROVIDED that at least **15% of estimated exploration expense** has been incurred by the Successful Bidder (Shaktibhumi).

**Phase 2:** After preparation of the Geological Report ("GR") until the **execution of the Mining Lease** or order by the Central Government under Section 11(1) of the CBA Act, 1957.

**What this means in plain terms:**
- **Right now (March 2026):** We are inside Phase 1. No change in control is permitted. Period.
- **After October 2026 (if 15% exploration cost spent):** There is a narrow window between Phase 1 ending and Phase 2 beginning (i.e., between the one-year mark and the preparation of the Geological Report) where a change in control MAY be permitted.
- **Once the GR is prepared:** Phase 2 lock-in begins and runs until the Mining Lease is executed — which could be **years** away. No change in control during this period either.

### 3.2 What Constitutes "Change in Control" (CMDPA Clause 1.1.22)

"Control" is defined as: *"the right to appoint majority of the directors or to control the management or policy decisions exercisable by a person or persons acting individually or in concert, directly or indirectly, including by virtue of their shareholding or management rights or shareholders agreements or voting agreements or in any other manner."*

This is an extremely broad definition. It catches not just majority shareholding but also:
- Shareholders agreements that give veto/consent rights on key decisions
- Board representation that gives effective management control
- Voting agreements
- "Any other manner" — a catch-all that the Nominated Authority can interpret broadly

### 3.3 What Happens if You Violate the Lock-in (CMDPA Clause 13.2)

**Clause 13.2.1:** The Nominated Authority or Central Government may, in its sole discretion:
- Appropriate the **Performance Security** (this is a large bank guarantee, typically crores)
- **Disqualify** Shaktibhumi from participating in any future coal auction
- **Terminate the CMDPA** entirely
- **Withdraw the Allocation Order** — meaning Shaktibhumi loses the coal block completely

**Clause 13.2.2:** Any transfer of right, title or interest which is not in conformity with the CMDPA or Applicable Laws **shall be deemed void *ab-initio*** — meaning it's treated as if it never existed. Shield would have paid ₹50 crore for shares that are legally nothing.

### 3.4 The Only Exceptions During Lock-in (CMDPA Clause 13.1.1 Proviso)

Change in control during the Lock-in Period is permitted ONLY if:
1. It is pursuant to an **insolvency proceeding** under the IBC 2016; OR
2. It is pursuant to **enforcement of security** by a bank or financial institution

Neither applies to Shield's proposed investment.

## 4. WHY THE CURRENT MOU IS ILLEGAL — SPECIFIC PROBLEMS

### Problem 1: Direct Equity Transfer During Lock-in Period

**The problem:** The MOU contemplates Shield receiving 49% equity in Shaktibhumi immediately or near-immediately after signing. The Allocation Order was issued October 10, 2025. We are currently in Phase 1 of the lock-in. Any share issuance that could constitute a change in control is prohibited.

**Why it matters:** Even if you argue 49% doesn't give "Control" because Shaktibhumi's promoters retain 51%, the MOU's governance provisions (reserved matters, board seats, veto rights) would almost certainly be interpreted by the Nominated Authority as giving Shield effective control or shared control over management and policy decisions. The definition of Control explicitly includes control via shareholders agreements.

**What could go wrong:** Shield pays ₹50 crore. Nominated Authority discovers the shareholding change (which it will, because Shaktibhumi must intimate changes and because CIN records are public). Allocation Order is withdrawn. Coal block is re-auctioned. Shield's shares are void ab-initio. ₹50 crore is gone. Shaktibhumi's Performance Security is forfeited. Both parties are potentially disqualified from future coal auctions.

### Problem 2: The "TO BE DISCUSSED" Authorized Capital (MOU Clause 9.1)

**The problem:** The MOU leaves the increase in authorized capital as "TO BE DISCUSSED." Under Section 29 of the Indian Contract Act, an agreement whose terms are uncertain is void. You cannot issue 49% equity to Shield if the authorized capital hasn't even been decided.

**Why it matters:** Without a defined authorized capital figure, there is no legal mechanism to issue the shares. The MOU creates a ₹50 crore obligation for Shield but no enforceable right to receive shares.

### Problem 3: Unfilled Lock-in Date Placeholder (MOU Clause 10.1)

**The problem:** Clause 10.1 references "post [mention date of lock-in open]" but the date is left blank. This is because nobody drafting this MOU actually checked when the lock-in ends — because under the CMDPA, it doesn't end on a fixed calendar date but depends on regulatory milestones (Mining Lease execution, GR preparation, etc.).

**Why it matters:** This reveals that the MOU was drafted without reference to the actual regulatory restrictions. The lock-in is not a simple fixed period that you can insert a date for — it's tied to operational and regulatory milestones that haven't been reached yet.

### Problem 4: PESA Compliance — Pakur-Dumka is a Scheduled Area

**The problem:** The coal block is in Pakur-Dumka district, Jharkhand. Significant parts of this area are **Scheduled Areas** under the Fifth Schedule of the Constitution, governed by PESA (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas Act, 1996). Under PESA, **Gram Sabha consent** is a legal prerequisite for any mining activity. This consent cannot be overridden by any government authority.

**Why it matters:** If Gram Sabha consent has not been obtained or is refused, the entire project cannot proceed. The MOU makes no mention of PESA compliance. Shield would be investing ₹50 crore into a project that may not legally be able to operate.

### Problem 5: Draft DPR on Tentative Conditions (MOU Clause 1.2)

**The problem:** The MOU acknowledges that only 20% of GR exploration is complete and the DPR is based on "tentative conditions." The ₹200-300 crore total project cost is explicitly stated as subject to change after full GR completion. Yet Shield's ₹50 crore investment and 49% equity stake are being priced against these tentative numbers.

**Why it matters:** Shield is paying a fixed price for an uncertain asset. If the full GR reveals the mine has less coal than estimated, or if the project cost balloons to ₹500 crore, Shield's 49% was overpriced. There is no valuation adjustment mechanism.

## 5. LEGALLY SOUND STRATEGY FOR SHIELD TO GET 49% FOR ₹50 CRORE

### RECOMMENDED APPROACH: Convertible Instrument with Deferred Equity Conversion

This is the gold standard approach used in regulated sectors where immediate equity transfer is restricted.

#### Structure:

**Step 1 — NOW (March 2026):** Replace the MOU with a binding **Investment Agreement** structured as follows:

- Shield provides ₹50 crore to Shaktibhumi as **Compulsorily Convertible Debentures (CCDs)**
- CCDs are debt instruments that mandatorily convert to equity upon occurrence of a specified trigger event
- **Trigger Event:** The earlier of (a) expiry of Phase 1 Lock-in (October 10, 2026, subject to 15% exploration spend condition), or (b) the date on which a change in Control becomes permissible under the CMDPA without requiring prior approval
- Upon conversion, Shield receives 49% of the enlarged share capital of Shaktibhumi
- During the debenture phase, Shield is a **creditor, not a shareholder** — there is no change in shareholding, no change in control, no violation of the lock-in

**Step 2 — DURING THE DEBENTURE PHASE:** Shield's rights as a debenture holder:

- **Security:** The CCDs are secured by a charge on Shaktibhumi's assets (excluding the coal block itself, which cannot be encumbered without CMDPA compliance)
- **Information Rights:** Monthly financial statements, quarterly progress reports on exploration, access to audit records — these are standard creditor rights, not control rights
- **Negative Covenants (Creditor Protections):** Shaktibhumi shall not, without Shield's consent: (a) take on additional debt above a threshold, (b) dispose of material assets, (c) change its constitutional documents, (d) declare dividends, (e) enter related party transactions above a threshold. These are creditor protections, not governance/control rights, and do not trigger "change in Control" under the CMDPA definition.
- **Observer Right:** Shield may appoint one observer (non-voting) to Shaktibhumi's board. An observer without voting rights does not constitute control.

**Step 3 — CONVERSION (Post Lock-in):** When the Trigger Event occurs:

- CCDs automatically convert to equity shares
- Shaktibhumi increases authorized capital and issues shares to Shield
- Shield receives 49% of the enlarged share capital
- Shaktibhumi gives 15 days' prior intimation to the Nominated Authority and Central Government per CMDPA Clause 13.1.1
- The SHA (Shareholders Agreement) is executed simultaneously
- **Critical:** The SHA must be structured so that Shaktibhumi's promoters retain "Control" as defined in the CMDPA — they keep the right to appoint majority of directors and control management/policy decisions
- Shield's governance rights post-conversion should include: board representation (minority seats), information rights, anti-dilution, tag-along, ROFR — but NOT majority board appointment or veto on operational decisions that could be interpreted as "Control"

#### Why This Works:

1. **No lock-in violation:** During Phase 1, Shield holds debentures (debt), not equity. No change in shareholding = no change in control. The Nominated Authority has no grounds to object.

2. **Shield's money is secured:** CCDs are secured instruments. If Shaktibhumi defaults, Shield has creditor rights including enforcement of security — which is actually one of the permitted exceptions to the lock-in under Clause 13.1.1.

3. **Conversion is legally compliant:** By the time CCDs convert, either (a) Phase 1 lock-in has expired, or (b) if the timing aligns badly with Phase 2, the SHA can be structured so that 49% without control does not trigger the restriction (since the restriction is on "change in Control," not on "change in shareholding").

4. **Companies Act compliant:** CCDs are well-recognized instruments under Companies Act 2013. The issuance requires board and shareholder approval, compliance with Section 42 (private placement) or Section 62 (preferential allotment), and prescribed valuation.

5. **FEMA compliant (if Shield has any foreign component):** CCDs are permitted instruments under FEMA for FDI. Coal mining allows 100% FDI under automatic route per Press Note 4/2019.

#### Timeline:

| Milestone | Estimated Date | Action |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Investment Agreement + CCD Issuance | April 2026 | Shield pays ₹50 crore, receives CCDs |
| Phase 1 Lock-in Expires | October 10, 2026 (if 15% exploration spent) | Window opens for conversion |
| CCD Conversion + Share Issuance | October-November 2026 | Shield gets 49% equity, SHA executed |
| Intimation to Nominated Authority | 15 days before conversion | Compliance with CMDPA Clause 13.1.1 |
| Geological Report Completion | TBD (could be 2027+) | Phase 2 lock-in begins — but if conversion happened before GR, Shield already holds equity |

**Critical Timing Point:** The narrow window between Phase 1 lock-in expiry and the preparation of the Geological Report is your conversion window. If the GR is prepared before October 2026, Phase 2 begins immediately and the window may not open until the Mining Lease is executed (which could be years). **Shaktibhumi must NOT complete and submit the GR before the CCD conversion happens.** This needs to be a contractual obligation in the Investment Agreement.

### ALTERNATIVE APPROACH: Subscription Agreement with Deferred Closing

If CCDs add complexity Shaktibhumi is uncomfortable with, an alternative:

- Sign a binding Share Subscription Agreement NOW
- Shield pays ₹50 crore into an **escrow account** managed by an independent escrow agent
- **Closing Condition:** The lock-in period has expired and change in Control is permissible under the CMDPA
- Upon satisfaction of Closing Condition, escrow releases funds to Shaktibhumi and shares are issued to Shield
- If Closing Condition is not satisfied within a longstop date (e.g., 24 months), funds return to Shield with agreed interest

This is simpler but gives Shield less protection during the waiting period (Shield is just a depositor, not a secured creditor).

## 6. ADDITIONAL REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS — MUST BE ADDRESSED

### 6.1 PESA / Gram Sabha Consent

Pakur-Dumka is in Jharkhand's Scheduled Area. Before ANY mining activity, Gram Sabha consent under PESA 1996 is a legal prerequisite. The Investment Agreement must include:
- Representation from Shaktibhumi that Gram Sabha consent has been obtained (or a condition precedent requiring it)
- Right for Shield to exit and recover its investment if Gram Sabha consent is refused or revoked

### 6.2 Environmental and Forest Clearances

The coal block requires Environmental Clearance (under Environment Protection Act, 1986) and Forest Clearance (under Forest Conservation Act, 1980) from MoEFCC. These are separate from the Allocation Order and can take 1-3 years. Shield's investment must be protected if these clearances are denied.

### 6.3 Companies Act Compliance for Share Issuance

When equity is eventually issued to Shield:
- Valuation by a registered valuer (mandatory under Companies Act Section 62 and FEMA if any foreign element)
- Board resolution and special resolution of shareholders
- Filing with ROC (Form PAS-3 for private placement, Form SH-7 for capital increase)
- Compliance with Section 186 if any inter-corporate loan/investment dimensions

### 6.4 Stamp Duty — Jharkhand

Jharkhand has specific stamp duty rates for share transfer and debenture instruments. Inadequately stamped documents are inadmissible in court. Verify current Jharkhand rates before execution.

## 7. NON-NEGOTIABLE RED LINES FOR SHIELD

Before proceeding with any investment, Shield must insist on:

1. **No equity during lock-in.** Use CCDs or escrow. Any MOU or agreement that purports to give Shield equity while the lock-in is running must be rejected — it's not just bad negotiation, it's illegal and void ab-initio.

2. **Contractual obligation to delay GR submission.** Shaktibhumi must agree not to complete and submit the Geological Report to the Nominated Authority until after CCD conversion. This preserves the conversion window between Phase 1 and Phase 2 lock-in.

3. **Full regulatory due diligence before money moves.** Shield must verify: (a) the Allocation Order is valid and subsisting, (b) the CMDPA has been executed, (c) Performance Security has been furnished, (d) all Eligibility Conditions continue to be met, (e) PESA / Gram Sabha status, (f) forest and environmental clearance status, (g) no pending litigation or disputes regarding the coal block.

4. **Security for Shield's investment.** During the CCD/escrow phase, Shield's ₹50 crore must be secured by a charge on Shaktibhumi's assets (excluding the coal block to the extent restricted by CMDPA). Personal guarantees from Shaktibhumi's promoters (capped).

5. **Exit right if conversion becomes impossible.** If the regulatory framework changes, or if Shaktibhumi's Allocation Order is revoked, or if the lock-in is extended indefinitely, Shield must have the right to demand full repayment of ₹50 crore plus agreed interest within 30 days, failing which Shield can enforce its security.

## 8. WHAT THE INVESTMENT AGREEMENT MUST CONTAIN

Replace the current MOU entirely with a properly drafted Investment Agreement containing:

1. **Recitals:** Full background including Allocation Order details, CMDPA execution, exploration status, regulatory framework acknowledgment
2. **CCD Terms:** Principal amount (₹50 crore), coupon rate (if any), conversion ratio (resulting in 49% equity on fully diluted basis), conversion trigger events, mandatory conversion timeline
3. **Conditions Precedent to Funding:** Satisfactory due diligence, Allocation Order validity certificate, CMDPA copy, board and shareholder approvals, regulatory certifications
4. **Representations and Warranties from Shaktibhumi:** Regarding legal status, coal block status, no regulatory violations, no pending proceedings, PESA compliance status, accuracy of exploration data, no undisclosed liabilities
5. **Affirmative Covenants:** Timely exploration per Mandatory Work Program, maintenance of Eligibility Conditions, compliance with CMDPA, regular reporting to Shield
6. **Negative Covenants (Creditor Protections):** No additional debt beyond threshold, no asset disposal, no change in business, no related party transactions without consent, no GR submission before conversion
7. **Conversion Mechanics:** Trigger events, timeline, share issuance mechanics, authorized capital increase, SHA execution
8. **Security Package:** Charge on assets, personal guarantees, pledge of promoter shares
9. **Events of Default:** Allocation Order revocation, CMDPA termination, material breach, insolvency, failure to maintain Eligibility Conditions
10. **Exit / Redemption:** If conversion becomes impossible, mandatory redemption at ₹50 crore + interest within 30 days
11. **Post-Conversion SHA:** Annexed in agreed form — board composition, reserved matters, information rights, anti-dilution, tag-along, ROFR, drag-along threshold, exit mechanism, deadlock resolution
12. **Governing Law / Dispute Resolution:** Laws of India; Arbitration under MCIA Rules or SIAC Rules; Seat of arbitration: Delhi or Mumbai (not Jabalpur or Ranchi)
